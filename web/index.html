Case Study · Consumer hardware

Mira Okafor spent nineteen months in a rented garage convincing nobody that her toothbrush was a good idea.

Then a stranger from a hardware fair in Shenzhen sent her a single text — “your hinge is wrong, but the rest is right” — and the next four years happened.

The first prototype, she will tell you without a hint of embarrassment, was glued together with the cheap epoxy that comes in the small yellow tube. It split on the third demo. She kept demoing anyway, holding the two halves together with her thumb, because the meeting after that one was the meeting that mattered, and she had already decided — in the way founders decide things — that she was not going to lose it for a reason as boring as glue.

What follows is the long version of that story, told the way she tells it at midnight, after the second drink, when she is no longer auditioning for anyone. The cap-table version exists elsewhere. This one is about the hinge, and the stranger, and the nineteen months that nobody writes about.

From the editor

We started this because the founder stories we kept reading felt like they had been ironed flat. Same arc, same lessons, same tidy quote near the end. We wanted the wrinkled version. The bit between the last failed pitch and the first one that worked, where the person was still mostly a person, and the company was mostly an idea, and the only thing in the room was a long quiet stretch nobody talks about because it doesn’t make for a good slide.

— The Editors, FIRSTHAND

FORM 01 · Subscription Issue No. 04

A long letter, every Sunday morning.

One case study, hand-edited, in your inbox. No referral schemes. No “five lessons from this week.” Just one founder, told well. You can leave whenever you want — we will not chase you.

Thank you. A confirmation note is on its way — check your inbox, and the promotions tab if it’s shy.

We send one letter on Sunday and a short note on Wednesday when something is worth saying. That is everything.

House rules
  1. i.

    Talk to the person, not the press release.

    Every study begins with at least four hours of recorded interview. The company arrives later, and only as far as it explains the person.

  2. ii.

    Cite everything we can.

    Where a date, number, or quote can be sourced, it is. The provenance lives in the footnotes — boring on purpose, and load-bearing.

  3. iii.

    Quiet beats loud.

    We are not interested in the loudest founder of the week. We are interested in the founder who is still around in seven years, and what that took.

  4. iv.

    The subject sees the draft.

    They cannot rewrite us, but they can correct us. We have made the call, more than once, to kill a piece because the person was no longer comfortable.
